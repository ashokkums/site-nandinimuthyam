Section 05 — The Artist

In Her Own Words

On stillness, listening, and the language found between Koothuppattarai and Bharatanatyam.

I have always been drawn to what exists beneath words. The pause before a response. The flicker in a gaze that gives something away. The quiet, persistent weight of what is felt… but never spoken.

My training in theatre, in the mic-less, body-led discipline of Koothuppattarai, taught me to listen differently.

Not to the voice alone, but to breath. To stillness. To the subtle ways the body reveals what the mind tries to contain.

Bharatanatyam deepened that listening. It gave form to emotion, and structure to instinct — where a glance can hold longing, and a single gesture can carry the weight of memory.

Somewhere between these worlds, I found my language.

As an actor, I am not interested in performing emotion as much as I am in allowing it to surface. In trusting that what is true does not need to be amplified. That stillness, when held with honesty, can be as arresting as movement.

My work lives in that in-between space

where the body remembers, where silence is not empty, and where the unspoken often tells the fuller story.
This is not something I put on. It is something I return to. Again and again. This is me, in motion.
